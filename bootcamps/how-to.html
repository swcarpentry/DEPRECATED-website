A boot camp is an intensive hands-on workshop during which participants learn software skills based on shorts tutorials and practical exercises. A typical boot camp runs over 2 days with 20-50 attendees, 2-3 instructors, and at least one helper for every 10 attendees.

The following page documents our best practices for organising and running a bootcamp. We've divided up this guide by the major roles involved in putting on a bootcamp:
  • Organisers plan the logistics bootcamp (facilities, staff, transportation, food, etc).
  • Instructors give the lectures, and lead and help the practical sessions.
  • Helpers assist instructors, typically by offering one-on-one assistance to bootcamp participants.

As an organizer of a bootcamp you are responsible for coordinating the entire event.

Initial planning (at least two months out)

This is stage in which you will finalize important decisions that will drive many of your other decisions: from the topics covered, to the room used, to how you plan any follow-up sessions.

  1. Decide whether the boot camp is open or closed?

    That is, can anyone apply or is it restricted to a specific university or company?

    Considerations: A closed bootcamp can be more easily tailored to a specific audience, the attendees are more likely to know one another and follow-ups will be easier. Beware that boot camps are so popular you will have to continuously resist people who will try to persuade you to open it up. If you've got good reasons to keep it closed or invite-only, keep it that way.

  2. Decide whether the boot camp is free?

    Decide whether the boot camp will be free, require a deposit (repaid if attendee is at the boot camp for both days) or have a small fee.

  3. Find/apply for funding

    Funding will allow you to pay for instructors' travel and lodging expenses (if necessary), food, and equipment.

  4. Recruit instructors

    You need at least two or three instructors for a two-day bootcamp. (It's too much to ask a single person to talk for eight hours a day for two days, and attendees will be more engaged if they hear from more than one person.) Ideally, at least one will already have co-taught a boot camp with someone else, and the others will either have taught or helped out.

    It's critical that you recruit your instructors before you advertise your boot camp. Ensure that you have back-up instructors who can take over if the original instructor has to pull out.

    Offer your instructors help in travel and finding accomodation. Ideally, you should offer to reimburse them their travel and accomodation expenses as this is how we help sustain Software Carpentry.

  5. Choose your dates

    Considerations:

    • The availability of instructors. This is likely your primary constraint.
    • Academic term. If you hold the boot camp at a university, keep in mind that the start of term works better than end of term (when exams are held), and that the start of second (or subsequent) term works better for graduate students than the start of first term (the start of an academic year is already busy).

      In our experience mid-term breaks and reading weeks have not worked well—they have had high no-show rates.

    • Proximity to events. You might also consider scheduling a boot camp immediately before or after a conference. Not only will this give you a large audience, but it will also make the boot camp easier to run, because it's easier to teach attendees who are all from the same community.

  6. Book a room

    You'll need to find a room with:

    • Front-facing tables so that attendees can watch an instructor and use their laptop at the same time.
    • Space for mingling without tripping over cables and squeezing between chairs. Lecture theatres are not recommended as their banked floors and fixed seating makes it very difficult for helpers to mingle.
    • High-speed wi-fi or network access that can withstand everyone using it at the same time.
    • Power for all the computers.
    • A microphone if the room's very big or has poor acoustics.
    • Disabled access.
    • Air-conditioning, because all those computers (and attendees) will generate a lot of heat.

    The boot camp will run even more smoothly if you have two projectors so that interactive demos can be shown alongside supporting slides, and also space for food and drink so that everyone can snack as they're working.

Two months before

  1. Create your timetable

    To start, check out previous boot camps for ideas on what lectures to cover. (All Software Carpentry materials are freely available under a Creative Commons licence, so you are free to re-use and re-mix the materials.)

    You can include time for a checking your environment session at the start of each practical, so that the attendees' environments and tool-sets are all checked and fixed as a group. This saves you from repeating the same thing individually to each attendee. Even if you ask attendees to prepare their environment before the boot camp, you will find that some have not and some cannot.

    Decide when you'll start and end each day, what you'll cover, and when breaks and lunch will be. A later start on the first day (e.g., 09:30 instead of 09:00) gives a bit of extra time to travel for anyone who doesn't live nearby. Similarly, an earlier end (e.g., 16:30 instead of 17:00) allows non-locals time to travel home. Attendees may have child-care needs so bear this in mind.

    For example, your time table might look like this:

    Day 1
    8:30 - 9:00 Setup Help
    9:00 - 10:30 The Shell
    10:30 - 12:00 Python Variables
    12:00 - 1:00 Lunch
    1:00 - 2:00 Python Data Structures
    2:00 - 3:00 Python Flow Control
    3:00 - 4:30 Python Functions and Modules
    Day 2
    9:00 - 10:30 Version Control Local
    10:30 - 12:00 Version Control Remote
    12:00 - 1:00 Lunch
    1:00 - 2:30 Debugging
    2:30 - 3:30 Testing
    3:30 - 4:30 Documentation

  2. Recruit helpers

    You'll need at least one helper for every ten attendees, and it's ideal to have one helper for every five attendees. Recruit helpers locally, from your own and neighbouring institutions.

    Offer your helpers help in travel and finding accomodation, if they need it.

    Helpers should have some knowledge of the material covered in the boot camp. Ideally, a helper will already have attended a boot camp and would like to become an instructor themselves.

  3. Set up registration

    Find out if your institution or venue expects you to use their in-house registration system. If so, use it! If not, then we recommend EventBrite, and Software Carpentry are happy to host registrations on their EventBrite site. Make sure you enable a waiting list, because boot camps are very popular.

    Boot camps have traditionally been free, but we are now experimenting with a small registration fee (e.g., $20) to reduce the no-show rate. (The money we raise is used to cover pizza, coffee, local travel expenses, and so on.) Be careful if you want to do this: as soon as you're charging anything at all, some venues will charge you for use of the space.

  4. Advertise

    Create a web page with information about the boot camp: what it is, when it will be held, a link to the registration page, and local travel information. It's best to keep this web page separate from the registration page so you have more control over both style and content.

    We can provide advice on how to advertise and are happy to publish your blog articles and tweets to our followers. We can also help to promote boot camps, either by advertising them or hosting your web page. If you are interested then please contact us by email at {{contact_email}}.

    If you plan to photograph, record, or broadcast your boot camp then mention this on the web page. A signed photo release may be required from each person if pictures are being taken.

    Tweet and blog about the event and send emails to departmental mailing lists, disciplinary mailing lists, specific people (e.g., lab directors or principal investigators), and anyone else who you think will be interested. Remember to include links to the advertising web page and the registration page as shown below:

    When: May 14-15, 2013, 9.00 am to 5.00 pm.

    Where: Room 99, Tesla Memorial Hall, Euphoric State University.

    What: Our goal is to help scientists and engineers become more productive by teaching them basic computing skills, like program design, version control, testing, and task automation. In our teaching we emphasise how these skills can contribute to quality, usable, reproducible research. In this two-day boot camp, short tutorials will alternate with hands-on practical exercises. Attendees will be encouraged both to help one another, and to apply what they have learned to their own research problems during and between sessions. There will be online follow up sessions for six to eight weeks extending the material from the boot camp.

    Who: The course is aimed at postgraduate students and other scientists who are familiar with basic programming concepts (like loops, conditionals, arrays, and functions) but need help to translate this knowledge into practical tools to help them work more productively.

    Requirements: Attendees must bring a laptop with a few specific software packages installed. (The list will be sent to attendees a week before the boot camp.)

    Content: The syllabus for this boot camp will include:

    • Using the shell to do more in less time.
    • Using version control to manage and share information.
    • Basic Python programming.
    • How (and how much) to test programs.
    • Working with relational databases.

    Registration: Please use this form to register your interest by April 6. We will allocate places to individuals and groups we believe will benefit, while aiming for a balance of subjects and geographical areas. We strongly encourage learners to attend with colleagues, so please create or join a team when you sign up. Ideally we would like teams of 3 to 6 members, and we will give preference to teams including one more knowledgeable member who is interested in organizing further training at their own institution.

    There is no charge to attend, however, by registering you are stating that you will attend both days of the boot camp and participate in the online exercises and follow-up sessions in the following weeks.

    We will notify all applicants as to whether they have a spot no later than April 13.

    Contact: For further information please email us at software-carpentry@euphoric.edu.

  5. Arrange catering

    It's great to provide coffee or snacks at breaks if you can get someone to pay for them. If not, make sure that there is somewhere nearby where attendees can purchase refreshments and schedule for this.

    If you are providing snacks, remember to allow for allergies and other dietary restrictions.

  6. Set up an email list

    Set up an email list so that you can inform everyone about the latest news and address any queries. You'll find this useful for organising online follow-ups after the boot camp too.

Two weeks before

  1. Publish your materials

    Make all your materials available to both the helpers and the attendees. Publishing your slides, code, and scripts to the helpers in advance allows them to know what's coming and to be able to help more effectively. For attendees, having access to the materials allows them to catch up in their own time if they fall behind. (The easiest option is usually to clone one of the Software Carpentry repositories on GitHub.)

  2. Publish attendee setup information

    Publish the list of software you expect attendees to have installed before they come to the boot camp.

    Our boot camp setup pages lists the standard boot camp software and has instructions how to install it all for various operating systems.

    You should also provide installation instructions for any software specific to your boot camp. Make sure you test your installation instructions on standard operating systems (Linux, Windows, Mac OS X) before you make them public.

    Add a link from your advertising web page to the setup page. Be clear that attendees are expected to set up their computers before arriving at the boot camp, and that they should email you if they have problems. (And by "before", we mean over a week before, not the day before or the evening before—this will save time on the day.)

    If your attendees are all local then you may want to consider having a drop-in setup surgery a couple of days before your boot camp.

  3. Select attendees

    Select the attendees you want to attend and inform them that they have a seat. Ask them let you know as soon as possible if they can't make the boot camp, so that you can offer their place to someone else.

    Add the selected attendees to your email list. Email the attendees and get them set up their computers as described on your boot camp's setup page.

    Apologise to the people who didn't make it into your selection, and let them know that they'll be held on a on a waiting list just in case an attendee drops out.

  4. Send out a pre-camp questionnaire

    You may want to consider sending out a questionnaire prior to the boot camp to assess the expertise and interest of the attendees, where they come from, what they're researching, and what operating system they'll be using. This allows you to customise what you present, how you present it, and the level at which you present it accordingly. For examples, see our articles on pre-assessment and post-assessment.

  5. Encourage instructors to do a dry-run

    Encourage instructors to do a dry-run before the boot camp to check that everything they have planned to teach is working. This is especially important if they'll be presenting commands live at a command-prompt.

  6. Gather your gear

    You will need the following gear for the boot camp,

    • A projector (preferably two so that interactive demos can be shown alongside supporting slides).
    • A MAC VGA adapter, for anyone who presents using a Mac.
    • Extension leads so that everyone can have access to a socket.
    • Two different colors of Post-It notes people can use to signal when they have finished an exercise or when they have problems, to answer multiple-choice questions, etc.
    • Guest wi-fi or network usernames and passwords for everyone.
    • If possible, workstations with the required software just in case someone can't get access to the wi-fi or network.
    • An attendance sheet so that you can see whether there are any no-shows.
    • Photo release forms, if you intend to take photos.
    • Garish badges for instructors and helpers.

Congratulations! You are almost there. The days leading up to the boot camp can be very full. We've organised the rest of the guide as a checklist to keep you on track:

One week before

  • Request confirmation from attendees that they will be attending.
  • Let the people on the waiting list know if they can attend or not.
  • You may want to invite ten or so locals from the waiting list to turn up. If there are still empty seats at mid-morning on the first day, then they're welcome to stay. If not, they're welcome to come back for the next one, but make it clear before they arrive, that their participation is not guaranteed.
  • Remind attendees of the setup page, and make it clear that they are expected to set up their computer before they attend the boot camp and to email the list if they have a problem.
  • Send a pre-boot camp questionnaire, if you want to find any information out beforehand (e.g. their understanding of programming, languages they want to work with, their research backgrounds).
  • Contact your instructors and ask them to make their material available and put any practical exercises on web pages so that attendees can refer to them during the boot camp. Data files or other useful information should also be added.
  • Contact your helpers, see if they have any questions and ask them to arrive 10-15 minutes early so they can be briefed.
  • Remind instructors and helpers to familiarise themselves with all the material (especially instructors, in case they end up covering for an instructor who has to pull out) and also to set up the boot camp software on their laptops too.
  • Publish your materials online if you have not already done so.
  • Identify a suitable pub or bar for socialising after the first day.
  • Invite your instructors and helpers to go out for dinner.

The day before

  • Is the building and the room going to be unlocked on the day of the event?
  • Is the room clearly signposted?
  • Do you know where the toilets are?
  • Is there any noisy construction/cleaning going on and, if so, can you do something about it?
  • Is the projector working, and do you have a spare bulb?
  • Do you have a MAC VGA adapter?
  • Do you have enough extension cables?
  • Is the network or wi-fi working?
  • Have you set up network or wi-fi guest accounts?
  • Do you have a contact number for maintenance and technical support?
  • Have you double-checked with catering?
  • Have you emailed a reminder to everyone (EventBrite can do this for you)?
  • Have you set up all the accounts, web sites and repositories you will need?
  • Do you have red and green sticky notes?
  • Do you have badges for instructors and helpers?
  • Print out the attendance sheets?
  • Print out photo release forms?
  • Print out feedback forms?
  • Do you have a watering hole to socialise in after the first day?
  • Have you arranged dinner for your instructors and helpers?

At the boot camp

Before you welcome everyone:

  • Put up signs directing attendees to the room.
  • Give instructors and helpers their badges.
  • Brief helpers on their role: mingle, answer questions, watch for body language and help attendees.
  • Ensure that helpers are seated around the room.
  • Make network connection instructions available on a hand-out or projector.
  • Give everyone a few minutes to plug-in, get on the network and settle in.

As part of your welcome:

  • Tell the attendees:
    1. who the instructors and helpers are and what they're there for
    2. what Twitter hash tag you're using
    3. where the bathrooms and snacks are
  • Circulate the attendance sheet and photo release form.
  • Give everyone a red and a green sticky note and explain what they are for.

At the close of the last day:

  • Circulate feedback forms
  • Remind attendees of any online follow-up sessions
  • Collect the attendance sheet and photo release form
  • Ask if any attendees would be willing to act as helpers or instructors at future boot camps. If so note down their contact details and forward them to us or please ask them to contact us by email at {{contact_email}} - we maintain a list of potential helpers and instructors which can help when organising future boot camps.

After the boot camp

The day after

  • Send out a feedback questionnaire, if you have one.
  • Remind attendees about the online follow-up sessions.
  • Remind attendees to get in touch if they'd like to help or instruct at a future boot camp or to organise one at their institution.
  • Write a blog post about how it all went, both good and bad points—be honest!

For Instructors

Your job is to give lectures and lead practicals. You should also mingle with the attendees during practical sessions and provide assistance.

Before

  1. Book transport to the boot camp location, and lodging. (Save your recipts!)
  2. Prepare your sessions(s).
  3. Review Mike Jackson's Top Tips for instructors
  4. Prepare the materials you'll need:
    • data sets
    • cheat sheets
    • exercises
    • repositories
    • etherpad notepads. You can set up as many etherpad notepads as you like. Stick to a sensible naming convention (e.g., bootcampname-date-lesson) so we can track down the etherpads later. Or create an account on etherpad.mozilla.org to automatically track the pads you create.
    • ..
  5. Do a practice run of your tutorials and practicals, especially if you'll be presenting commands live at a command-prompt.
  6. Ensure you have the same setup as the attendees by working through the boot camp software set-up pages.
  7. Post your practical exercises on web pages to allow students to refer to them during the boot camp. Data files or other useful information should also be added.
  8. Familiarise yourself with the material your fellow instructors will be presenting, just in case one of them pulls out.

On the day

  1. If you are using multiple windows (e.g., a command window and an editor window) then you'll need to ensure that they are both of sufficient size to be visible by all attendees, or pause before switching from one window to the other, because some attendees may get confused when you switch windows.
  2. Use different background colors for different shell windows, and encourage students to do the same. This makes it easier to follow the window you are using (but keep in mind red-green and blue-yellow color blindness when choosing colors).
  3. As you type at the command-line, read out what you're typing.
  4. Back up the material with your own anecdotes, experiences and evidence—it prevents the lectures from being too dry.
  5. Emphasise the reasons why good software development skills contribute to good, reproducible, reusable research.
  6. For any question with a binary answer (yes/no, have done/have not done, works/does not work), ask your attendees to hold up their green (yes) or red (no) sticky notes so that you can gauge their level of understanding.
  7. When you've asked attendees to do something, get them to put a red sticky note prominently on their laptop and to replace it with a green one when they've succeeded/completed. This lets you gauge how the attendees are progressing.

The day after

  1. Send the organisers a list of what you think worked and what didn't, to help improve future boot camps.

What Not To Do

No discussion of how to teach a boot camp would be complete without some discussion of what not to do. To that end, here are a few common mistakes.

Bore them.

Your audience will never care more about what you're teaching than you appear to, so if they get the feeling you're not interested in it, they won't be either. This does not mean you have to shout, crack three jokes a minute, or harangue them about how this stuff is really, really important, but you do owe it to your audience to show up mentally as well as physically.

Ignore them.

You're not there to reproduce one of our online videos in person: you're there to interact with people so that they get a better learning experience. You shouldn't ever go more than two or three minutes without asking a question (and listening to the answer), and if it has been 15 minutes since any of your learners asked one, odds are you've either lost them or are boring them.

Let them ignore each other.

Done right, Software Carpentry boot camps are a great networking opportunity for our learners (and for us, too). If you don't know half your learners by name at the end of two days, you're doing it wrong; if they haven't each made at least half a dozen new contacts, that's your fault too. Have them work in pairs, and require them to mix up the pairs at least a couple of times. Equally, encourage them to chat to one another at coffee breaks and lunch, and to get a pizza or some curry together for dinner on the first day.

All talk, no action.

The more time folks spend with their hands on the keyboards doing exercises, the more time they're actually paying attention. The students have their computers in front of them: if you talk for more than five minutes without asking them to use their computers, they'll do so anyway—on Facebook.

Use magic.

Typing too fast, using shortcuts or commands they haven't seen yet—basically, any time you say, "Don't worry about this just now," or they say, "Wait, how did you do that?" or, "Can you please slow down, I can't keep up," you're no longer actually helping them.

Don't close the loop.

You can't optimize without feedback, so:

  1. Give them all red and green sticky notes so that they can put up a green one when they're finished an exercise, and a red one when they have a question or need setup help.
  2. Have them fill in "minute cards" before each break, i.e., write down in one sentence something they've learned or found useful, and something that confused them or didn't work for them, on the two sides of a small card, and hand that in.
  3. Make sure to leave at least 20 minutes at the end of the final day for feedback (whether it's a verbal "one good/one bad" report, or filling in an online survey, or whatever else you're using).

And remember: feedback is pointless if you don't pay attention to it (or worse, if you explain it away). There's no point using minute cards if you don't bother to read them until the boot camp is over, and no point collecting feedback after each boot camp if you don't change what and how you teach to reflect it.

Don't master the material yourself.

It's OK to make mistakes and not know things—in fact, stumbling in front of your learners and then picking yourself up is a great teaching opportunity. But if they believe you don't have a solid grasp of the concepts underlying the material you're teaching, they will have less confidence in everything you say, even when you're right.

Don't tell them "why".

Most of our learners are graduate students in science and engineering, so they know what evidence looks like, and why working practices should be evidence-based. That doesn't mean you have to have the whole of empirical software engineering at your fingertips in order to teach, but please do read Facts and Fallacies of Software Engineering and sprinkle a few of the findings it quotes into your lessons.

Show them the forest but not the trees.

The things we teach reinforce each other, so tie them together at every opportunity. Point out that connecting things with pipes in the shell is like chaining functions together, or that they can use a shell script to re-run a bunch of different tests before committing to version control, and so on. If possible, take 15 minutes or so each day to show them how you use these tools in your day-to-day work.

Underestimate setup time.

Something always fails to install for someone, or a bunch of learners are accidentally locked out of the building after lunch, or whoever was supposed to drop off power bars didn't. Roll with it, and remember to laugh (even if it's a bit hysterically).

Underestimate setup requirements.

Do you have enough power outlets? (Are you sure?) Do you have enough bandwidth to handle fifty people hitting your version control repository at the same time? (How do you know?) Can everyone actually log in? And while we're here, does campus security know you're using the room over the weekend?

For Helpers

As a helper, your job is to help the attendees during practical sessions. Helpers typically have attended a previous boot camp, or are familiar with the subject matter.

Before

  1. Ensure you're familiar with or have read over the material the instructors will cover in the sessions you're helping with.
  2. If you have time, try and work through the setup information for the boot camp before you come along. Many of the questions you may get as a helper on the first day will be on installation/post-installation problems.
  3. Read the blog posts by Katy Huff and Aleksandra Pawlik on how to be a helper.

On the day

  1. Go round the room and look for attendees who need help. They may be holding up a red sticky note, but they may also just be sitting there looking despondent or peering intently at their laptop.
  2. If you can't answer an attendee's question, just say so and grab an instructor.

The day after

  1. Send the organisers a list of what you think worked and what didn't, to help improve future boot camps.
